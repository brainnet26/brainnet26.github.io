Cornelis Jan Stam

The Clinical Neurophysiology of Brain Networks: towards an integrated view on brain disease
Recently there has been a rapidly growing understanding of disorders of the central nervous system accompanied by the emergence of novel treatments, pharmacological as well as based upon (non) invasive brain stimulation. This has been accompanied by an urgent need for improved early disease diagnosis, monitoring and guidance of (non) invasive treatment. Clinical Neurophysiology techniques such as EEG, MEG, and electro-corticography (ECog) have played an important role here. This is particularly true for the study of large-scale brain networks in neurological disorders. That the brain is a complex network of interconnected and interacting specialized local brain regions has been known at least since the pioneering work of Cajal. However, a number of discoveries in graph theory at the start of the millennium have revolutionized our understanding the nature, function and breakdown of complex networks. Importantly, techniques from clinical neurophysiology such as EEG, MEG and ECoG allow a unique, dynamic view on brain networks (Stam 2014; Stam and van Straaten, 2012). Such studies have shown that healthy brain networks are characterized by an optimal organization of local connectedness, global integration, hierarchical modularity and the presence of highly connected “hub” regions. A key finding with clinical implications for diagnosis as well as treatment is that hub regions are highly vulnerable to damage in a wide range of neurological disorders such as Alzheimer’s disease, multiple sclerosis, brain tumors, stroke and epilepsy. This “hub overload and failure” may constitute a final common pathway for many central nervous system disorders, and is starting to guide novel approached to assessment of prognosis and guided (non) invasive brain stimulation (Stam, 2023).

Key words: Clinical Neurophysiology, Brain networks, EEG, MEG, Alzheimer’s disease, Multiple sclerosis, Brain tumors, Stroke, Epilepsy
References:
Stam C.J. (2023) Hub overload and failure as a final common pathway in neurological brain network disorders. Network Neuroscience, in press. [https://doi.org/10.1162/netn_a_00339]
Stam C. J. (2014). Modern network science of neurological disorders. Nature reviews. Neuroscience, 15(10), 683–695.
Stam, C. J., & van Straaten, E. C. (2012). The organization of physiological brain networks. Clinical neurophysiology : official journal of the International Federation of Clinical Neurophysiology, 123(6), 1067–1087.



Sarah Vandenbulcke

Modelling MR elastography to assess the mechanical impact of microstructural variation in the brain
Mechanical stiffness has been proposed as a marker for neurodegeneration because of the observed reduction in brain stiffness in disorders such as Alzheimer’s and Parkinson’s disease. While magnetic resonance elastography (MRE) enables non-invasive measurement of this property, the underlying microstructural changes remain poorly understood. Multidimensional diffusion imaging provides information about sub-voxel brain microstructure. This research investigates how microstructural variation, measured using multidimensional diffusion imaging, relates to brain stiffness using finite element models. The aim is to improve our understanding of brain mechanics and explore new approaches for measuring the brain’s mechanical properties.

Alex Roxin

If you learn, they will drift: why we should expect neuronal representations to change over time
Long-term recordings have revealed that neuronal representations slowly change over time across the cortex even when associated behaviors are well-learned and highly stable. Plasticity-driven changes in synaptic connectivity can reproduce these effects in network models, including the oftentimes complex and nontrivial role of experience on drift rates. Interestingly, despite changes in single-cell activity, the topology of the neural manifold is invariant over time in experiments. This property is reproduced in network models with Hebbian plasticity, but also in deep networks trained to categorize images, suggesting it may be a general feature of learning systems, both biological and artificial.

Rikkert Hindriks

Algebraic invariants for detecting broken detailed balance in time-domain electrophysiological brain signals
Methods from statistical physics have recently been used to study cognitive processes, consciousness, and neurological disorders. In particular, temporal irreversibility measures have been applied to electrophysiological signals to detect non-equilibrium brain dynamics. However, due to volume-conduction, it is beneficial to use measures that are invariant under invertible linear transformations of the sensor signals. We introduce an algebraic framework to systematically derive such measures for time-domain electrophysiological signals and apply them to detect broken detailed balance in electrocorticography (EcoG) signals recorded during general anaesthesia.


Moritz Helmstaedter

Cerebral Cortex Connectomics
The mapping of neuronal connectivity is one of the main challenges in neuroscience. Only with the knowledge of wiring diagrams is it possible to understand the computational capacities of neuronal networks, both in the sensory periphery, and especially in the mammalian cerebral cortex. Our methods for dense circuit mapping are based on 3-dimensional electron microscopy (EM) imaging of tissue, which allows imaging nerve tissue at nanometer-scale resolution across substantial volumes, extending to more than one millimeter on the side, followed by AI-based image analysis to obtain dense connectivity maps, or connectomes. With these we have mapped local circuitry in mouse and human cortex, determining learning-related synaptic traces, inhibitory axonal development, and discovering an expanded interneuron-to-interneuron network in the human cortex. Most recently we completed the connectomic reconstruction of a cortical column. We are currently screening cortical connectomes across age, disease states and experience to obtain a deeper understanding of their relevance for individual behavioral performance and brain pathology.


Madeleine Skeppås

Reconstructing the single-cell spatiotemporal dynamics of glioblastoma invasion
Glioblastoma invasion into healthy brain tissue remains a major barrier to effective treatment, yet its spatiotemporal dynamics are difficult to quantify at single-cell resolution in a scalable and patient-specific manner. Here we introduce GlioTrace, an ex vivo imaging and computational framework for real-time tracking of glioblastoma invasion in patient-derived xenograft brain slices. By combining whole-specimen confocal microscopy with machine learning–based morphology classification and probabilistic modeling of state transitions, GlioTrace reconstructs how tumor cells switch between distinct invasion phenotypes over time and how these dynamics are shaped by local tissue context and therapeutic perturbation.

Tomoki Fukai

Crucial roles of inhibitory circuits in offline memory processing
The brain encodes what it learns through experiences into assemblies of tightly linked neurons and reactivates these assemblies for memory consolidation. In a spiking network model, I will demonstrate that neuron-specific inhibitory plasticity, compared with blanket inhibition, increases the diversity of reactivated assemblies. Our model suggests that a small overlap between two assemblies suffices for their sequential reactivations, thus nesting successive assembly reactivations. Further, I will provide a mechanistic framework for replay dynamics to clarify how the hippocampus anchors replay sequences to behaviorally salient locations. Our results predict distinct roles of inhibitory neuron subtypes in this process.


Paolo Medini

Cell type-specific inhibitory microcircuits function in (multi)sensory processing and plasticity in vivo
In many brain circuits, including the neocortex, inhibitory neurons are a minority, yet cell type–specific microcircuits are determinants of developmental and lesion-driven plasticity, multisensory integration, and sensory gating. Building on my work on visual cortical plasticity, where inhibitory synapse development sets critical period timing, I will discuss research on synaptic inhibition in interareal and interhemispheric interactions and multisensory processing in primary and association cortex and olfactory bulb. I will conclude with recent findings on cell type–specific inhibition in prefrontal sensory gating, disrupted in psychosis-prone disorders, and the therapeutic potential of chemo-/opto-genetic targeting with fewer side effects.


Ramón Nartallo-Kaluarachchi

Nonequilibrium physics of brain network dynamics
The dynamic activity of neurons and neural populations shows evidence of time-irreversibility suggesting that the brain operates in a nonequilibrium stationary state, and the level of irreversibility appears to be a signature of cognition and consciousness. In this talk, I will introduce the mathematical background of nonequilibrium stochastic dynamics in both continuous and discrete state-spaces. I will present techniques for applying this physical theory to the analysis of neural data across scales. Finally, I will discuss the functional implications of nonequilibrium dynamics for neural computation.


Sofie Ährlund-Richter

Prefrontal Cortex Feedback Reshapes the Representation and Routing of Visual Information
How does the brain determine which sensory signals matter and route them across cortical networks? Although prefrontal cortex (PFC) feedback is thought to shape sensory processing, its circuit-level mechanisms remain unclear. Here, we show that distinct PFC subregions differentially modulate visual processing in primary visual cortex (VISp). Using axonal tracing, two-photon calcium imaging, and projection-specific chemogenetic perturbations in behaving mice, we find that prefrontal feedback is not a uniform gain signal, but acts through modular pathways shaped by internal state and behavioral context. We are further developing a spectral unmixing–based two-photon approach to image defined VISp output populations simultaneously, enabling real-time observation of how feedback redistributes visual information across cortical channels. Together, this work defines circuit principles of top-down sensory control and provides a framework for understanding how disrupted feedback may alter perception and cognition in neurodevelopmental disorders.
